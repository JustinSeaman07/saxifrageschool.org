The Pittsburgh Forum

We're happy to announce the first annual Pittsburgh Forum, an academic residency focused on the intersection of theory and practice. The PghForum will be launching on the East End of Pittsburgh from August 9-12, 2013. This year's weekend-long academic residency is themed on “Our Life in Schools”. We will gather together and ask big questions on the past and present purpose of education, and how that purpose should inform its ongoing reform. The discussion on school will framed by this basic design method: 'The "How?" must come from the "Why?"'

Come talk, eat, read, listen, and work with 15 colleagues and ask these important questions concerning our lives in schools.

See our participant list here.

The Experience

The Pittsburgh Forum is designed to be an immersive intellectual experience for participants featuring a series of guest speakers and small community projects, as well as allotted time for personal reading and reflection in addition to participant reflection presentations. This program will mirror a traditional academic residency in rigor and thoughtfulness while intentionally expanding the experience to include hands-on projects and activities that reflect both the theory and practice of our theme.

For most of us, we spent 20+ years in school always with an eye torward the future. We want to address, head on, the tension between "life" and "life in school". Is the schooled life really life? Is it just preparation to live better after we graduate? How do we reconcile or understand the distinction between school and the rest of life? During this short residency will ask these questions on the purpose of education and the relationship between school and life. We will engage with a number of texts, a range of guest speakers, human-centered design sessions, and work on a small neighborhood project. Residents will live together for 4 days in a large Victorian home in Pittsburgh’s historic Highland Park neighborhood.

Registration

Participants from a diverse range of experiences, backgrounds, and skill sets are encouraged to apply. Whether you are student, education professional, or merely share a passion for these issues, we invite you to join us for this residency. Most everyone has spent 20+ years in schools, we invite you to spent a few days and wonder “Why School? How?”. If you are interested, please fill out an application. You will be notified of your acceptance within approximately two weeks. Registration is limited to 16 spots.

Like all things we do at the Saxifrage School, we're keeping costs to an absolute minimum. Participant fees for the entire weekend will be $195. A $50 deposit is required to confirm registration. You will be eligible to register if your application is approved. The program will be hosted through a generous partnership with the Pittsburgh Urban Leadership Service Experience (PULSE) in their large Victorian home on Stanton Ave.

What You Get

Your costs go to cover four days of lodging, planned activities, speaker fees, learning materials, administrative costs, and all meals from Friday afternoon through Monday morning (August 9-12). Required Reading List

Please submit your recommendations when you apply to join the residency.


More texts will be released shortly.

1. Civic Engagement in Education

The keynote address at the recent Digital Media and Learning (DML) conference. Ethan Zuckerman is director of the Center for Civic Media at MIT, and a principal research scientist at MIT's Media Lab. His research focuses on the distribution of attention in mainstream and new media, the use of technology for international development, and the use of new media technologies by activists. You can follow his thoughts at EthanZuckerman.com and on Twitter at @EthanZ.

2. Thoreau's Walden

This seminal work gets to the heart of the distinction between school and life, between preparation and what it means to truly live. Though flawed in both theory and practice, Thoreau's writing at Walden asks "Why?" as good as any: "I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately, to confront only the essential facts of life, and see if I could not learn what it had to teach, and not, when I came to die, discover that I had not lived. I did not wish to live what was not life" Excerpts from Chapters 1 & 2.

3. The One World School House

Whether you love it or hate it new online platforms are changing the structure of American education. In the years to come, lives in schools may look very different in no small part to this man, Sal Khan. His platform, the Khan Academy, has opened a series of fascinating and important questions about the sharing of educational content and the mapping of knowledge.

4. Shop Class as Soulcraft.

a recently popular book on the value of manual labor over purely theoretical knowledge. Author Michael Crawford (who has a background working in think tanks and and as a fellow in the University of Virginia's Institute for Advanced Studies in Culture while also owning an operating a motorcycle repair shop) makes an impassioned case for shop class and blue collar work instead of what he sees as an educational system focused on churning out "knowledge workers" or "symbolic analysts" who sit at desks and deal in only in ideas. He calls for the more students and workers to achieve self-mastery through "hard disciplines" like gardening, learning a language, or learning to properly clean an engine.

More (Optional) Reading...

  • David Nasaw, Schooled to Order.

    A brief history of American schooling that focuses on three central periods: the Common School reforms of the 1830s & 40s; the rise of secondary education in the early 20th century; and, the massification of education after WWII. The premise is that American schooling has always been primarily focused on control and secondarily focused on education.

  • David Tyack, The One Best System.

    This is a narrative of the struggle of urban education systems to try and respond to the explosion of children in late 19th and early 20th century immigration. Overwhelmed, systems turned to bureaucracy and efficiency as ways to deliver their schooling.

  • Joel Spring, The Sorting Machine.

    This book follows the power of the testing movement based in academic psychology as a way to justify the placing of children in various levels of the schooling apparatus. (Spring has written widely on a number of other related topics as well.

  • John Gatto, Dumbing Us Down


  • Neil Postman, The End of Education
