About the Class

In this 12-week course for beginners, you will learn the theory and practice of graphic design. Practiced content will include a broad technical introduction to the Adobe Creative Suite (Photoshop, Illustrator, InDesign); the Theory content will focus on learning design principles, as well as discussions on aesthetics, excellence, and media. Students will do a lot of collaborative and project-based learning and create a small portfolio of work done for real clients. Our hope is to de-mystify the design process and the use of professional software tools. We'll teach you a lot, but the main goal is to give you the resources and motivation you need to learn on your own.

Software Options

In order to offer the most valuable experience, we are going to teach the class using the industry standard software, the Adobe Creative Suite (CS): Photoshop, Illustrator, and InDesign. This software, however, can be quite expensive so here are some suggestions to help you navigate the software acquisition. The best scenario is that you get a new copy of CS6 that you can use during and after the class. Here's the list of options:

1. Buy CS6 Design Standard

Very expensive, costs about $1300. If you are currently a student at an accredited University(and have a photo ID) you can get a discounted price.

2. Have your employer sponsor you

Many employers are interested in the professional development of their staff, especially when it comes to ultra-useful things like Graphic Design. See if they have a copy of Adobe CS you can use, or if they will buy one for you. Non-profits can get heavily discounted copies ($150) at www.techsoup.com.

3. Have a non-profit sponsor you

Is there a non-profit for whom you will be doing design work? If so, you could ask them to either let you use their copy of Adobe CS or they could get a discounted copy from TechSoup.com ($150) and let you use it for the course. If your non-profit is getting the software through Tech Soup, start the process now because it can take a few weeks to get approval.

4. Creative Cloud Subscription

Adobe has a relatively new subscription option for the CS6 software that gives you software access and offers Cloud Storage for design files. There's a few options here:

a. Use a free 30-day Cloud trial, then pay for 2 months at $75/month. Total cost: $150
b. Use a free 30-day Cloud trial, then pay for 1 month of Illustrator ($30) and then change subscription and pay for 1 month of InDesign ($30). Total Cost: $60.
c. Use a free 30-day Cloud trial, then pay for a year's subscription to the Creative Cloud at $50/Month. Total cost: $600.
d. If you already have one of the CS programs but not the others (maybe you have Photoshop, but not Illustrator and InDesign), you could just do a subscription for whatever you do not have.

5. Buy an Older Version

While not as ideal, older versions of Adobe CS Design Standard (Photoshop, Illustrator, and InDesign) will be compatible with our course. As with any software, the older the version, the less capability...but we think anything CS3 and up should work just fine. Each of these older versions is able to be upgraded (for a lower cost) to the new version, should you want to in the future. Make sure to buy the correct version (Mac or PC) Options: Buy CS 5.5 for $1,050; Buy CS 4 for $750; Buy CS 3.3 for $325. Of course you can always check e-bay for copies of these older versions.

6. Use Open Source Options

In general, we do not recommend this option! We will be teaching from the Adobe programs in class.

Much like OpenOffice, there are some free software options available made by Programmer Super Heroes just for you. This is only recommended for the extremely adventurous, computer-savvy user. Read this article for a brief explanation on how they compare. Remember, we are teaching with Adobe tools, so you may have to translate between differing menus if you use a non-adobe like open source tool.

1. GimpShop is a solid option as a Photoshop replacement. (Curious? Read reviews) Supposedly GimpShop's interface is identical to Photoshop, so menu translation shouldn't be an issue..
2. Inkscape seems like the best option for an Illustrator replacement, but its interface is different and may require some translation.
3. The Open Source In Design alternative is called Scribus It too likely has a non-adobe-like interface.
